CHAPTER 0
The door sealed with its usual, smug little kiss. The
metronome in the corner ticked like a patient woodpecker.
The window—square of pale—minded its business.
“You don’t begin with one,” he said, already sweeping the
table with his palm as if truth were a spill. “Begin with what
holds. What do you see?”
“Nothing,” I said.
“Lazy,” he said. “Say what you see.”
“Grain. A ring where a cup sat too long. Light. A place to
put something.”
“Good. Affordance. Invitation. ‘Nothing’ is the most hard-
working employee in the room; you just don’t pay it.”
He dragged out a ledger with a green cloth spine. Every
zero had been punched out with a knife until the columns
were a lace of neat holes. “Balance last month.”
I tried. The numbers bucked where the zeros should have
steadied them. He watched with the bored relish of a man
who knew exactly when I’d fall off and preferred to let
gravity handle it.
2 JAKE AARON
“You’re drowning in a saucer,” he said, not unkindly. “Pull
the placeholder and you don’t get purity. You get slapstick.”
A plain white bowl appeared. He poured water to the
brim until the meniscus dared the room. One tap and a drop
fled down the porcelain like a guilty thought.
“Zero isn’t denial,” he said. “It’s the room. The margin
that makes a word possible. The bowl that turns ‘wet’ into
‘soup’ instead of a crime scene.”
He stilled the metronome with two fingers. The ache
where the click should have landed rose like a held breath.
“The rest performs,” he said. “Silence is time’s punctua-
tion. No marked gap, no cadence. No cadence, no count. No
count, no meaning. ‘Nothing’ is grammar, not a ghost. Try to
eat a paragraph without spaces and see how that treats you.”
“Inhale,” he said, eyes on my throat. “Longer out than in.”
I obeyed. I matched his count more than his breath, but
the room softened all the same.
“Good girl,” he said, offhand, as if handing a dog a
ribbon. Somehow it still burned.
A small jar appeared: one white marble against glass.
Another followed, packed tight with identical marbles.
“Which has one?”
“The first.”
“Why?”
“Because I can see it.”
“No.” Clean as a blade. “Because there is room. The space
that is not marble makes this marble legible. In the stuffed jar,
‘one’ is everywhere and nowhere—no edge, no count. Call it
perfection and you’ve reinvented non-being with better PR. A
crown needs the hole where the head goes. A letter needs
paper. A joke needs timing. This is not controversial.”
“Isn’t this just wordplay?” I said. “‘Nothing’ obviously
means a lack.”
“Exactly. Lack-of-what, lack-for-what. Relation, not syrup.
Ontic modesty—try it on, it fits everyone.”

“Vacuum?”
“Geometry, fields, fluctuations. A very busy ‘nothing.’”
“Silence?”
“Noise floor in costume. Also scored rest. Musicians pay
for it on purpose.”
“Absolute zero.”
“A rumor with good math. Even ground states twitch.”
He reached for my wrist as if to take my pulse and found
instead a steadiness that made him narrow his eyes. What-
ever hypothesis that sparked, he filed it without comment. He
is careful with hypotheses the way surgeons are careful with
fingers.
“You think this makes life small,” he said. “It doesn’t. It
makes it honest. If you can’t love the bowl, you can’t be
trusted with the soup. If you can’t count the spaces, don’t
write poetry. If you can’t hear the rest, don’t pretend to keep
time.”
He tapped the metronome back into motion. Tick. Tick.
The arm cut light, then light again, sewing.
“When you lose this,” he said, walking me to the door,
“clear a surface. Listen for the ache where the click should
fall. Put zeros back in your counts. Breathe longer out than in.
The big rhythm gathers the little ones.”
He rested a hand on my shoulder for the length of one
beat and let go. I straightened the chair, wiped ghost-chalk
from the board, and left the door ajar so the tick could leak
into the hall like a lighthouse with manners.
Later, trying to name the feeling, I failed and settled for a
sequence: first the sting, then the steadiness, then the odd
relief of being held by something that wasn’t a hand. Margins
before pictures. Grammar before song.

CHAPTER 1 — The House Without a Door
The latch made that small, contented click it always made, as
if the door were pleased with itself for doing its job again. The
metronome worked a slower seam through the air. He had
already squared the chair, leveled the table with a touch only
he would notice, and found the one speck of dust that had the
nerve to exist.
“Today,” he said, “we quit pretending there’s a fire escape.”
He set a plain card in the center of the table. A sentence had
been typed on it, flat as a fence plank. “Read this silently,” he
said. “Don’t blink at me like a hostage. Just read. When you’re
done, tell me what you felt.”

“The window,” I started.
“Glass. Room. Light. All ours,” he said. “Try again.”

CHAPTER 2 — The Held Shake
He had turned the table into an instrument.
A single string ran from clamp to clamp across a narrow
board, a movable bridge set loose beneath it. He plucked once
with a nail and the room filled with a clean line of sound that
made the water in the bowl quiver.
“This,” he said, “is existence without manners.”
He touched the string again, softer, then slid the bridge to the
middle and pressed. The pitch leapt an octave; the segment
between bridge and clamp blurred into a stillness that wasn’t
still.
• • •
MY BOOK 13
“Watch the quiet part,” he said.
I watched the quiet part. It trembled like a lie.
“Perfect stillness,” he went on, “is a rumor with a good tailor.
Everything that holds, holds by moving. If it looks like a
statue, it’s a standing wave trying not to grin.”
He flicked the string at different spans until the air wore
stripes. At certain places, the flour he’d dusted on the board
gathered into neat little dunes while other places went bare.
“Nodes,” he said, tapping a dune with the back of a knife.
“Antinodes.” He dragged a fingernail across the string; the
sound went sour, then sweet. “Identity is a way of changing
that remembers itself.”
He had put two metronomes on a thin plank and set them
slightly out of phase. The first tick came like a dropped pin;
the second answered late, then earlier, then late again, until
both snapped into lock as if embarrassed to be caught flirting.
“Entrainment,” he said. “Two strangers on a loose floorboard
become a choir given a minute and a draft.”
I reached for the nearest metronome to nudge it free of the
other and his hand arrived on mine as if it had been waiting
there in a future I hadn’t met yet.

CHAPTER 3 — The Ladder and the Sign
He had replaced the string with a pair of small speakers and a
microphone wired back into itself. “Governance,” he said, as
if introducing a villain. He turned the knob and the room
bloomed with a low, pleasant hum, then a soft howl, then a
banshee shriek that made the porcelain on the shelf disagree
with being alive.
I flinched. He clicked something invisible and the scream
died to a well-behaved purr, like a cat remembering who pays
rent.
“Same gain,” he said. “Different sign. Positive hands the
system a megaphone and tells it secrets. Negative tells it to sit
down and try again. You don’t get stability by pep talk.”
